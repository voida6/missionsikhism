## The founding

Guru Nanak Dev Ji was born in 1469 at [Rai Bhoi Ki Talwandi](/events/birth-of-guru-nanak), now Nankana Sahib. His teaching was radical in its simplicity: one God, the equality of all people regardless of caste or creed, honest work, and service to others. After years of travel he settled at [Kartarpur](/events/founding-of-kartarpur), where the first Sikh community took shape around three practices that still define Sikh life — devotion (*nam*), honest labour (*kirat*), and the shared kitchen (*langar*).

## Nine Gurus follow

Leadership passed through nine further Gurus. Guru Angad standardised the Gurmukhi script; Guru Amar Das strengthened the langar and challenged social inequities of his day; Guru Ram Das [founded Amritsar](/events/founding-of-amritsar) around its sacred pool. Guru Arjan [compiled the Adi Granth](/events/installation-of-adi-granth) and installed it in the Harmandir Sahib in 1604 — and two years later became [the first Sikh martyr](/events/martyrdom-of-guru-arjan).

## Sword and spirit

In response, Guru Hargobind took up temporal as well as spiritual authority — the doctrine of *miri-piri* — and raised the [Akal Takht](/events/founding-of-akal-takht) opposite the Harmandir Sahib. Guru Tegh Bahadur [founded Anandpur](/events/founding-of-anandpur) and was [executed in Delhi in 1675](/events/martyrdom-of-guru-tegh-bahadur) for defending the freedom of others to practise their faith. A generation later, the four Sahibzade — the sons of Guru Gobind Singh — were killed in 1704: the elder two in battle at Chamkaur, and the younger two, still children, bricked alive at Sirhind.

## The Khalsa and eternal Guruship

Guru Gobind Singh [created the Khalsa](/events/creation-of-the-khalsa) at Anandpur on Vaisakhi 1699, giving the community its distinctive identity and code. Before his death at Nanded in 1708 he ended the line of human Gurus, [vesting eternal Guruship in the Guru Granth Sahib](/events/guru-granth-eternal-guruship).
