## The march north

Commissioned by [Guru Gobind Singh](/people/guru-gobind-singh) at Nanded in 1708, Banda Singh Bahadur crossed into the Punjab carrying the Guru's arrows, a drum and a standard, and letters calling the Sikhs to arms. Peasants, artisans and dispossessed cultivators rallied to him through 1709–10 as towns east of the Sutlej fell. His object was Sirhind and its governor Wazir Khan — the man who had ordered the younger Sahibzade, the Guru's two youngest sons, bricked up and executed in 1704.

## The battle

On 12 May 1710, on the plain of [Chappar Chiri](/places/chappar-chiri) outside Sirhind, Banda's irregulars met and broke Wazir Khan's professional army of cavalry, artillery and war elephants. Wazir Khan was killed in the fighting; two days later Sirhind was taken and sacked. The Sikh chronicles remember the battle as retribution delivered; the Mughal chronicles record the shock of a provincial capital falling to men the empire had hunted as outlaws.

## The first Sikh state

From the fort of Lohgarh, Banda [struck coin and a seal](/history/sovereignty-banda-singh) not in his own name but in the name of the Gurus, and revenue records suggest he broke up the zamindari estates of the region, conferring proprietorship on the cultivating peasants — among the earliest such reforms on record in India. Sovereignty lasted barely six years before [the empire closed in](/events/martyrdom-of-banda-singh), but the precedent — that the Khalsa could rule — outlived him and animated [the misls](/history/misl-period) and [the empire of Ranjit Singh](/history/sikh-empire).
