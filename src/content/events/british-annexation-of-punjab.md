## Ten years of unravelling

Ranjit Singh died in 1839; the succession devoured itself. Two Maharajas and several regents died violently in five years while real power passed to an army increasingly beyond civil control. In 1845 the Khalsa army crossed the Sutlej and the First Anglo-Sikh War followed — hard-fought at Mudki, Ferozeshah and Sobraon, and shadowed, the histories record, by treachery in the Sikh high command. The Treaty of Lahore (1846) took the Jullundur Doab, placed a British Resident at Lahore, and — by separate sale — transferred Kashmir to Gulab Singh Dogra for five million rupees.

## The second war and the proclamation

Revolt at Multan in 1848 rekindled the war. Chillianwala in January 1849 cost the British dearly, but Gujrat the following month broke the Khalsa army, and on 29 March 1849 the Governor-General's proclamation annexed the Punjab outright. The boy Maharaja Duleep Singh — [Ranjit Singh's](/people/ranjit-singh) youngest son — was pensioned, separated from his mother Jind Kaur, and eventually taken to England; the Koh-i-noor diamond passed to the British Crown.

## The last kingdom conquered

The Punjab was the final major Indian power to fall to the Company, ending the only large state Sikhs have ruled. Its administration became a British showpiece, and within a decade Sikh soldiers were a mainstay of the colonial army. The community's next great movement would be not military but [reformist](/events/founding-of-singh-sabha) — the [Singh Sabha era](/history/singh-sabha-reform) opens there.
