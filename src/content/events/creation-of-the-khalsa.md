## The call for a head

The assembly at [Anandpur](/places/anandpur) that Vaisakhi was vast — the springtime gathering when the community came to its Guru. The tradition's account is among the most dramatised scenes in Sikh memory: the Guru emerged sword in hand and asked for a Sikh willing to give his head. One by one, five volunteers followed him into the tent — Daya Ram, Dharam Das, Himmat Rai, Mohkam Chand, Sahib Chand — and each time the Guru re-emerged with a bloodied blade, until the five stood revealed alive, robed as the Panj Pyare, the Five Beloved Ones. Historians note the janam-sakhi-like layers in the telling; none doubt that the institution founded that day remade the community.

## Amrit and the double initiation

The five were initiated with *khande di pahul* — nectar stirred with a double-edged sword — and then, in the act the tradition treasures most, the Guru knelt and took initiation from his own initiates. Male initiates took the name Singh ("lion"), female initiates Kaur ("princess"); caste names were set aside. The initiated were bound to the discipline of the Five Ks and a code of conduct, ancestors of the modern [Rehat Maryada](/faith/rehat-maryada) and the [amrit ceremony](/faith/ceremonies-and-rites) as practised today.

## Sovereignty in a community's hands

The [Khalsa](/glossary/khalsa) — "the pure", or in the Persian revenue idiom, land held directly by the sovereign — placed the community under no intermediary lordship at all. Nine years later the Guru completed the design, [vesting eternal Guruship in scripture](/events/guru-granth-eternal-guruship) and leaving the Khalsa itself as the Guru's body in the world.
