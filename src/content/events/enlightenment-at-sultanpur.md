## The steward of Sultanpur

In his late twenties Nanak worked in Sultanpur Lodhi as steward of the granary and stores of Daulat Khan Lodi, the local Afghan governor — respectable, settled employment arranged through his brother-in-law. The janam-sakhis describe a disciplined routine in these years: work by day, and devotional singing with his Muslim companion Mardana before dawn.

## Three days at the river

Tradition holds that one morning, aged about thirty, Nanak entered the river Bein to bathe and did not emerge. Given up for drowned, he reappeared on the third day and, after a silence, spoke the words remembered as the charter of the faith: "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim." The janam-sakhis describe the experience as being carried into the divine presence and charged with the ministry of the Name. McLeod and later historians read the account as the community's memory of a genuine turning point, whatever its literal details — from this moment Nanak taught as one commissioned.

## The journeys begin

Nanak left employment, distributed his possessions, and set out on the great journeys — the *udasis* — that tradition extends over some two decades, east to Bengal and Assam, south toward Sri Lanka, north into the Himalaya, and west as far as Mecca and Baghdad. The message he carried is treated across [the Faith section](/faith/foundations-philosophy): [one God](/faith/one-god), no caste before the divine, honest living, and remembrance of the Name. The journeys ended where settled community began — [at Kartarpur](/events/founding-of-kartarpur).
