## A throne facing the temple

The building Guru Hargobind raised in 1606 — with, tradition records, only Baba Buddha and Bhai Gurdas permitted to work on its construction — was a deliberate piece of architecture-as-doctrine. Facing the Harmandir Sahib across the precinct, the Akal Takht ("throne of the Timeless One") housed the community's worldly business: petitions heard, disputes settled, envoys received, weapons and horses accepted as offerings. Spiritual sanctuary and temporal seat stood in one compound, in sight of each other.

## Miri and piri

At his succession the young Guru wore two swords, one for spiritual authority (*piri*) and one for temporal (*miri*) — the doctrine of [miri-piri](/glossary/miri-piri) that the Takht embodies in brick. From its platform the Guru reviewed the community's first armed retainers, and later Takht custodians issued *hukamnamas* — directives to the scattered congregations. Some sources date the founding 1606, others up to 1609; the site's meaning is not in dispute.

## The community's parliament

Through the eighteenth century the Takht was where the scattered warbands of the [misl period](/history/misl-period) gathered as the Sarbat Khalsa to pass collective resolutions (*gurmatas*) — the closest the persecuted community had to a parliament. It remains the senior seat among the five Takhts of the faith, which is why its shelling in [June 1984](/events/operation-blue-star) was felt as a wound to Sikh sovereignty itself, and why it was rebuilt by the community's own hands.
