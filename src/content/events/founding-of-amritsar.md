## A pool in the forest

The fourth Guru chose a site in central Punjab, between the Ravi and Beas, where a pool of water lay amid forest. Under Guru Ram Das the excavation of the tank began around 1577, and a settlement — Ramdaspur, "the city of Ram Das" — grew beside it, seeded by traders and artisans the Guru invited. Traditions differ on the land itself: one records purchase from the villagers of Tung, another a grant made in the time of the emperor Akbar. What is certain is that the town became the faith's commercial and spiritual anchor within a generation.

## Nectar gives the city its name

The completed tank was named Amrit Sarovar — the "pool of nectar" — and from it the city took the name [Amritsar](/places/amritsar). Under [Guru Arjan](/people/guru-arjan) the Harmandir Sahib was raised at the pool's centre, deliberately low and open on four sides, and in 1604 the newly compiled scripture was [installed within it](/events/installation-of-adi-granth).

## Centre of the Sikh world

From then on the city's story tracks the community's: [the Akal Takht raised opposite the Harmandir](/events/founding-of-akal-takht) in 1606, desecrations and rebuildings through the eighteenth century, gilding under [Ranjit Singh](/people/ranjit-singh) that gave the Golden Temple its English name, [the Singh Sabha's founding](/events/founding-of-singh-sabha) in 1873, and the tragedies of [1919](/events/jallianwala-bagh-massacre) and [1984](/events/operation-blue-star). No place holds more of this history in one square mile.
