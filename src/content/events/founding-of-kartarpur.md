## From wanderer to householder

When his journeys ended, Guru Nanak did something deliberate: he put on the clothes of a householder, took up farming on the right bank of the Ravi, and gathered a community around him. Kartarpur — "the town of the Creator" — became the working demonstration of his teaching. The choice mattered doctrinally as much as practically: against the renunciant traditions of his day, the Guru taught that liberation is pursued within ordinary life, not by abandoning it.

## The pattern of a Sikh day

The Kartarpur community kept a rhythm the faith still recognises: rising before dawn for meditation on the Name (*nam japna*), the day given to honest labour (*kirat karni*), and food shared without distinction of caste in the common kitchen (*vand chhakna*, expressed in the [langar](/glossary/langar)). Hymns were sung morning and evening — the beginnings of [kirtan](/glossary/kirtan) as congregational worship. It was here, the tradition holds, that compositions including the Japji took the form later fixed in scripture.

## Succession, not inheritance

Guru Nanak died at Kartarpur in 1539. Before his death he passed over his sons and installed his devoted follower Lehna as successor, renaming him Angad — "of my own limb." The act established the principle that Guruship followed merit and spirit, not blood, and it carried the young community through [nine further successions](/history/guru-period). Kartarpur itself, now just inside Pakistan, returned to the world's attention when [the corridor to it opened in 2019](/events/kartarpur-corridor-opens).
