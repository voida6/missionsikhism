## A community under new pressures

Colonial Punjab confronted the Sikhs with challenges no army posed: Christian mission schools winning converts, the Arya Samaj's *shuddhi* campaigns re-absorbing Sikhs into Hinduism, and census categories that made "who is a Sikh?" an administrative question. The immediate spur, the histories record, was the announced conversion of Sikh students at a Amritsar mission school in 1873. Leading Sikhs — Thakur Singh Sandhanwalia and Baba Khem Singh Bedi among the founders — established the Sri Guru Singh Sabha at [Amritsar](/places/amritsar) that year to answer with education and reform rather than reaction.

## Tat Khalsa

A second Sabha at Lahore in 1879, more radical in temper, carried the movement across the province. The reformers — the Tat Khalsa, "the true Khalsa" — worked to strip Sikh practice of accreted Hindu custom, arguing the case in tracts, newspapers and the scholarship of men like Kahn Singh Nabha, whose title *Ham Hindu Nahin* ("We are not Hindus") became the era's watchword. Khalsa College, Amritsar, founded 1892, and a network of Khalsa schools gave the movement institutional weight, and the Punjabi press it built carried [Gurmukhi literacy](/culture/language-and-script) into the countryside.

## Reform becomes self-rule

The movement's logic culminated after 1920 in the Gurdwara Reform (Akali) movement's campaign to wrest the shrines from hereditary custodians, the founding of the SGPC, and the Sikh Gurdwaras Act of 1925 — and its definitions of practice flowed into the modern [Rehat Maryada](/faith/rehat-maryada). Modern Sikh institutional life is, to a large degree, [the Singh Sabha era's](/history/singh-sabha-reform) creation.
