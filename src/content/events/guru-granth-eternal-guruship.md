## The last camp at Nanded

Guru Gobind Singh had travelled south with the emperor Bahadur Shah's camp in 1707–08, pressing the case for justice over the Punjab's governors, and settled at [Nanded](/places/nanded) on the Godavari. There he was stabbed by two Pathan assassins — set on, the chronicles hold, by Wazir Khan of Sirhind, who had most to fear from the Guru's influence at court. The wound, first sewn, reopened; the Guru knew his death was on him.

## "The Granth is the Guru"

Before his death in October 1708 the Guru performed his final institutional act: he placed the ceremonial offerings before the Adi Granth, bowed to it, and declared the line of human Gurus closed — the Guruship vested forever in the scripture, thereafter the [Guru Granth Sahib](/faith/guru-granth-sahib), and in the collective community, the Guru Panth. The tradition preserves the command in the couplet *Guru maneyo Granth* — "acknowledge the Granth as Guru." Takht Sri Hazur Sahib at Nanded marks the place.

## A succession no one could seize

The decision answered the problem that had shadowed every succession since 1539: rival claimants splitting the community. There could be no eleventh Guru to contest, capture, or corrupt. Authority passed to a book whose canon [had been fixed a century earlier](/events/installation-of-adi-granth) and a community [constituted at Vaisakhi 1699](/events/creation-of-the-khalsa). Weeks before his death the Guru had also commissioned [Banda Singh Bahadur](/people/banda-singh-bahadur) to carry the struggle north — [the next era](/history/sovereignty-banda-singh) begins there.
