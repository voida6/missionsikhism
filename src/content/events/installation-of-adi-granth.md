## Fixing the canon

By the fifth Guru's time, hymns of the Gurus circulated alongside imitations put about by rival claimants to the Guruship. Guru Arjan's response was editorial: working at Ramsar on the edge of Amritsar with Bhai Gurdas as scribe, he gathered the compositions of the first five Gurus and set them in order by musical measure (*raag*). Alongside the Gurus' own words he included the hymns of Hindu and Muslim saints — Kabir, Farid, Namdev, Ravidas among them — a canon-making decision with no parallel in the scriptures of the era: sanctity was recognised across community lines.

## Installed above, not beside

On 16 August 1604 the completed volume was carried in procession and installed in the newly finished Harmandir Sahib. Baba Buddha, the aged disciple who had known every Guru since Nanak, was appointed first *granthi* (custodian-reader). Tradition records that Guru Arjan placed the Granth on a raised platform and slept on the floor below it — the scripture, not the Guru, occupied the seat of honour.

## Toward eternal Guruship

The Adi Granth ("first book") was expanded a century later at Damdama, when the ninth Guru's hymns were added, and in 1708 Guru Gobind Singh [vested the Guruship itself in the volume](/events/guru-granth-eternal-guruship). What Sikhs revere today as the [Guru Granth Sahib](/faith/guru-granth-sahib) is the book installed that day in 1604 — the centre of every [gurdwara](/glossary/gurdwara) since.
