## Amritsar under the Rowlatt Act

The spring of 1919 found the Punjab in agitation against the Rowlatt Act, which extended wartime powers of detention without trial. In Amritsar the deportation of two local leaders, Dr Satya Pal and Dr Saifuddin Kitchlew, brought crowds into the streets; days of violence followed, and the city was placed under military control with public assembly banned — a ban many in the Vaisakhi crowds, swollen with villagers in town for the festival and the horse fair, had no way of knowing.

## Ten minutes of fire

On the afternoon of 13 April, Brigadier-General Reginald Dyer marched some fifty riflemen to Jallianwala Bagh, a walled garden with essentially one usable exit, where thousands were gathered. Without warning or order to disperse, the troops fired into the crowd for about ten minutes — some 1,650 rounds — ceasing, by Dyer's own account, only as ammunition ran low. The official inquiry counted 379 dead and over a thousand wounded; the Indian National Congress's inquiry put the dead near a thousand. Dyer told the Hunter Commission his purpose had been "a moral effect."

## The empire loses the Punjab's trust

Punjabis of every faith died in the garden, which stands minutes from the Golden Temple. The massacre — and the honours some in Britain paid Dyer after it — broke a loyalty the province had carried through the Great War, and it radicalised a generation across India. In 1940 Udham Singh, who claimed the garden's memory, shot the province's former Lieutenant-Governor, Michael O'Dwyer, in London. The Bagh is preserved today as a national memorial, its walls still bullet-scored — one of the [modern era's](/history/singh-sabha-reform) defining sites of memory.
