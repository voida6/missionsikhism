## The shrine visible across the border

After 1947, [Gurdwara Darbar Sahib at Kartarpur](/places/kartarpur) — raised where Guru Nanak farmed, taught and died — lay four kilometres inside Pakistan, while the bulk of the community lived in India. For seven decades pilgrims stood at the border village of Dera Baba Nanak and viewed the white dome through binoculars mounted for the purpose; proposals for access surfaced repeatedly from 1999 but foundered on the two states' hostility.

## An agreement across a closed border

In November 2018 both governments broke ground on a dedicated corridor, and on 9 November 2019 — days before the 550th anniversary of [Guru Nanak's birth](/events/birth-of-guru-nanak) — the crossing opened: a fenced, visa-free passage from Dera Baba Nanak to the shrine, admitting thousands of Indian pilgrims daily by permit. The first official *jatha* included political leaders from both states; the shrine itself had been vastly expanded for the occasion. That two governments without diplomatic normality built it — and reopened it after pandemic closure in 2021 — made the corridor a rare instance of the sacred overriding the political.

## What it means

For Sikhs the corridor restored bodily access to [the site where the first community took shape](/events/founding-of-kartarpur) — the completion, in a small way, of a geography [severed in 1947](/events/partition-of-punjab). It stands, so far, as the [modern era's](/history/modern-era) most hopeful entry: the border unmoved, but for pilgrims, for a few hours at a time, unmade.
