## The siege of Gurdas Nangal

The empire's response to Sirhind was total. Through 1715 imperial armies pressed Banda's diminishing force into the fortified haveli of Gurdas Nangal, near Gurdaspur, and starved it for eight months — the garrison reduced, the chronicles record, to eating boiled leaves and the flesh of their horses. In December 1715 the position fell, and Banda was taken alive with several hundred companions.

## The procession and the executions

The prisoners were marched to [Delhi](/places/delhi) in a deliberately staged procession — Banda caged, his followers on camels, the heads of the fallen borne on spears. Through the spring of 1716 the captives were executed in public batches, around a hundred a day by contemporary account, each offered his life for conversion; the sources on both sides record that virtually none accepted. In June 1716 Banda himself was executed with extreme torture, his young son killed before his eyes. Contemporary Mughal and European observers — the East India Company's agents among them — recorded the prisoners' composure with astonishment.

## Persecution, and persistence

The state followed the executions with a price on Sikh heads, driving the community into the hills and deserts — [the darkest decades](/history/misl-period) of the eighteenth century, remembered alongside [the great massacre of 1762](/events/vadda-ghallughara). Yet the institutions survived: the Khalsa's bands regrouped as misls, gathering [at the Akal Takht](/events/founding-of-akal-takht) to act in council. Banda's six years had shown Sikh rule was possible; the following fifty proved it could not be extinguished.
