## The emperor's own words

The execution is unusually well documented for its period: the emperor Jahangir recorded in his memoir, the Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri, his irritation at the Guru's growing following and his order that the Guru be dealt with. The immediate charge concerned the rebel prince Khusrau, who had passed through the Punjab and, it was alleged, received the Guru's blessing. A crushing fine was levied; the sources record the Guru's refusal to pay it.

## Death at Lahore

Guru Arjan was taken to [Lahore](/places/lahore) and died in Mughal custody at the end of May 1606, after torture that Sikh tradition recounts in detail — seated on a heated iron plate, burning sand poured over him — before his body was given to the Ravi. Gurdwara Dera Sahib, beside Lahore Fort, marks the site. He was the first of the Gurus to die for the faith, and the tradition remembers his composure in the words of his own hymn: "Sweet is Your will."

## The turn to self-defence

The martyrdom changed the community's posture permanently. His son and successor, [Guru Hargobind](/people/guru-hargobind), girded two swords — [miri and piri](/glossary/miri-piri), temporal and spiritual authority — and [raised the Akal Takht](/events/founding-of-akal-takht) opposite the Harmandir. The pattern set in 1606 — peaceful faith, met with state violence, answered by organised resilience — recurs through [the rest of the Guru period](/history/guru-period) and beyond.
