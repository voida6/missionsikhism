## The appeal from Kashmir

In 1675 a deputation of Kashmiri Brahmins, led in the tradition's account by Kirpa Ram, came to [Anandpur](/places/anandpur) seeking protection from the conversion pressure of the emperor Aurangzeb's administration. The Guru's response, as the Sikh chronicles preserve it, was to stake his own life: let the emperor be told that if Tegh Bahadur could be persuaded, the Pandits would follow. He set out for Delhi and was arrested on the way.

## Chandni Chowk

Brought in chains to [Delhi](/places/delhi), the Guru was pressed to perform a miracle or accept Islam, and refused both. Three companions were executed before his eyes — Bhai Mati Das sawn in two, Bhai Dayala boiled, Bhai Sati Das burned — and on 11 November 1675, by the traditional dating, the Guru was beheaded in the open square of Chandni Chowk. Gurdwara Sis Ganj Sahib stands at the spot; Gurdwara Rakab Ganj marks where his body was cremated by a follower who carried it away under cover of dust-storm, burning his own house around it.

## A martyrdom for another's faith

What the community holds singular here is that the ninth Guru died defending the religious freedom of a community not his own — remembered in the epithet *Hind di Chadar*, "the shield of India." The martyrdom hardened his nine-year-old son, soon [Guru Gobind Singh](/people/guru-gobind-singh), toward the resolution that produced [the Khalsa](/events/creation-of-the-khalsa): a community that could never again be made to choose between faith and life without the means to defend both.
