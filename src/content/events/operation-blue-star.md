## The road to June 1984

The early 1980s brought escalating confrontation between the Indian government and the Sikh political movement centred on the Anandpur Sahib Resolution's demands for greater Punjab autonomy — a confrontation that turned violent as the preacher Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale, accused by the government of directing militant killings, moved with armed followers into the Golden Temple complex and, from December 1983, fortified the [Akal Takht](/events/founding-of-akal-takht) under the direction of a former army general, Shabeg Singh.

## The operation

On the night of 5–6 June 1984 — with the complex crowded with pilgrims for the martyrdom anniversary of [Guru Arjan](/events/martyrdom-of-guru-arjan) — the Indian Army assaulted the temple precinct. Resistance was far stronger than anticipated; tanks were brought onto the parikarma and fired on the Akal Takht, reducing much of it to rubble. The government's White Paper recorded 83 soldiers and 493 militants and civilians killed; independent and Sikh estimates run considerably higher, some into the thousands. The Sikh Reference Library, with its manuscripts and relics, was destroyed by fire after the fighting — the circumstances disputed to this day. The Harmandir Sahib itself was scarred but survived.

## The aftermath

The storming of the faith's central shrine was experienced by Sikhs worldwide — including many with no sympathy for militancy — as a desecration. Sikh soldiers mutinied in several regiments; on 31 October 1984 the Prime Minister, Indira Gandhi, was assassinated by two Sikh bodyguards, and organised massacres of Sikhs followed in Delhi and other cities, with official inquiries recording some three thousand dead in Delhi alone. A decade of insurgency and counter-insurgency followed in the Punjab. The Akal Takht was rebuilt — twice, the second time by the community's own *kar seva*. The events of 1984 remain the [modern era's](/history/modern-era) deepest wound, and their memory a live presence in Sikh life [worldwide](/culture/global-community).
