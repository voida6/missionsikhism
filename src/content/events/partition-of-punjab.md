## A line through the homeland

The Radcliffe Award, published on 17 August 1947, divided the Punjab between the new dominions of India and Pakistan along a line drawn in five weeks by a commission whose chairman had never before been east of Paris. For Sikhs the geometry was uniquely cruel: the community's population lay scattered across both halves, its richest canal-colony farmland in the west, and its holiest ground — [Nankana Sahib](/places/nankana-sahib), [Kartarpur](/places/kartarpur), Panja Sahib, [Lahore](/places/lahore) itself — fell to Pakistan while [Amritsar](/places/amritsar) fell to India.

## The exchange of populations

What followed was among the largest and swiftest migrations in recorded history. In the Punjab alone some ten million people crossed the new border in both directions in a matter of months, in foot columns miles long, by rail and by river, through massacre, abduction and epidemic; estimates of the dead across the Partition run from two hundred thousand to a million or more. Virtually the entire Sikh population of West Punjab — well over two million people — abandoned homes, lands and shrines for the Indian side.

## Rebuilding east of the line

Resettlement consolidated the community in Indian East Punjab, where refugee cultivators broke in new land and the decades after brought the Green Revolution and, in 1966, a Punjabi-speaking state. Partition also seeded the [global diaspora](/culture/global-community), as displaced families sought futures abroad. The border it drew still divides the sacred geography of the faith — softened, for one shrine at least, by [the corridor opened in 2019](/events/kartarpur-corridor-opens).
