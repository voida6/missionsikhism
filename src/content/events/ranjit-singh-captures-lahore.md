## A nineteen-year-old and an open gate

By the late 1790s Lahore was held — misruled, the chronicles agree — by three Bhangi sardars, and the city's notables looked for a deliverer. Ranjit Singh, the young chief of the Sukerchakia misl, moved on the city in July 1799 with the connivance of leading citizens who opened the Lohari Gate, and with the backing of his formidable mother-in-law Sada Kaur of the Kanhaiya misl. The city fell with hardly a battle. He was nineteen.

## Maharaja of the Punjab

On Vaisakhi 1801 Ranjit Singh took the title Maharaja — while striking his coinage, like [the misls before him](/events/sikh-capture-of-lahore), in the name of the Gurus rather than his own. From [Lahore](/places/lahore) he set about absorbing the remaining misls and the Punjab's frontier provinces: Amritsar in 1802, Multan in 1818, Kashmir in 1819, Peshawar's tribute from 1823 and its annexation in 1834 — an empire [treated fully in Era 4](/history/sikh-empire), with a modernised army drilled by European officers at its core.

## The boundary with the British

In 1809 the Treaty of Amritsar fixed the Sutlej as the boundary with the East India Company, freeing the Maharaja to expand north and west while binding him to peace eastward — a peace that held for his lifetime. The empire he built from the 1799 capture survived him by only a decade before [the British annexed the Punjab](/events/british-annexation-of-punjab) in 1849.
