## The prize of the Punjab

Lahore was the seat of Mughal — and then Afghan — power in the Punjab, the city the Sikhs had been broken against for half a century. In 1765, with Ahmad Shah Durrani's grip loosening after each costly invasion, the Bhangi misl chiefs Gujjar Singh and Lehna Singh, with Sobha Singh, occupied the city — the same year the community, gathered at Amritsar, resolved its affairs as the Sarbat Khalsa. Durrani returned once more, but the occupation held in substance: the misls, not the empire, now commanded the Punjab's capital.

## Coin struck in the Gurus' name

The act that gave the capture its meaning was monetary. The misls struck the rupee later called the Nanakshahi, bearing the Persian legend the Khalsa had used since Banda's seal: *deg o tegh o fateh* — "kettle, sword, victory" — sovereignty claimed not for any chief but for the Gurus, echoing [the precedent set at Lohgarh](/events/battle-of-chappar-chiri) fifty-five years earlier. In the coinage of the age, whoever's name the money bore was king; the money named [the Gurus](/history/guru-period).

## From warbands to rulers

The capture marks the [misl period's](/history/misl-period) turn from survival to governance: revenue in place of raiding, the *rakhi* protectorate system maturing into administration, and a dozen misl families ruling from the Indus to the Jumna. The arrangement's weakness — twelve sovereignties where one was needed — was resolved a generation later when a Sukerchakia chief named Ranjit Singh [took Lahore for himself in 1799](/events/ranjit-singh-captures-lahore).
