## The column at Kup

Ahmad Shah Durrani's sixth invasion of India in early 1762 had a declared object: the destruction of Sikh power. Near Kup, outside Malerkotla, his rapid-marching cavalry caught the community's moving column — not an army in line but the *vahir*, the baggage train of an entire people: families, elderly, children, herds. The fighting men formed a moving cordon around the non-combatants and fought a running battle across open country through the day of 5 February 1762, dying in place as the column crawled toward safety.

## The toll

Estimates of the dead vary with the source — commonly cited figures run from ten thousand to upwards of twenty-five or thirty thousand killed in the single day, a large share of the entire community. Sikhs remember it as the Vadda Ghallughara, the Great Massacre, distinguishing it from the "lesser" massacre of 1746. Later the same year Durrani blew up the Harmandir Sahib and defiled the sacred pool with slaughtered cattle.

## Recovery within the year

What the sources register next is the speed of recovery. Within months the misls were raiding to the gates of Sirhind and Lahore; by autumn 1762 the community reassembled at Amritsar in Durrani's despite, and in 1764 Sirhind itself fell to the Khalsa. Three years after the massacre the misls [took Lahore](/events/sikh-capture-of-lahore). The Ghallughara stands in Sikh memory beside [the martyrdoms of the Gurus](/events/martyrdom-of-guru-arjan): catastrophe absorbed, not submitted to — the temper celebrated in the community's [martial tradition](/culture/martial-tradition).
