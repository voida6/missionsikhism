## What it is

Unlike the other daily prayers, Ardas (from the Persian *arz-dasht*, a written petition) is not a fixed composition of the Gurus enshrined in scripture: it is the community's own standing prayer, offered on the feet, palms joined, facing the [Guru Granth Sahib](/faith/guru-granth-sahib). Its wording — standardised in outline by the [Rehat Maryada](/faith/rehat-maryada) — grew with the community's history and still admits the occasion's particular petitions at its close.

## How it moves

It opens invoking the ten Gurus in order — the first lines drawn from the tradition of [Guru Gobind Singh's](/people/guru-gobind-singh) court — then remembers the community's costliest treasures: the martyrs, [the tortured and the unbroken](/events/martyrdom-of-banda-singh), those who kept the faith "to the last hair and the last breath," the shrines [severed by the border of 1947](/events/partition-of-punjab). It ends asking wellbeing for everyone: *Nanak naam chardi kala, tere bhane sarbat da bhala* — in the Name, ever-rising spirits; in Your will, the good of all.

## When it is offered

Ardas closes congregational worship and nearly every [ceremony and rite](/faith/ceremonies-and-rites) — naming, marriage, cremation, the opening and settling of the scripture — and Sikhs offer it privately at any undertaking or thanksgiving. The congregation answers each movement with *[Waheguru](/glossary/waheguru)*.
