## What it is

The Japji ("recitation") stands first in the [Guru Granth Sahib](/faith/guru-granth-sahib): the Mool Mantar — the faith's credal opening, beginning [Ik Onkar](/faith/one-god) — followed by thirty-eight stanzas (*pauris*) and a closing couplet the tradition attributes to Guru Angad. Composed by [Guru Nanak](/people/guru-nanak), it is recited rather than sung, and is assigned to no raag — it stands outside the musical scheme that orders the rest of the scripture.

## What it asks

Its governing question arrives in the first stanza: *kiv sachiara hoiai* — how is one to become truthful, how is the wall of falsehood broken? The answer unfolds through meditation on the Name, the limits of ritual and cleverness, the ordering power of divine command (*hukam*), and, in the closing movement, the five ascending realms (*khands*) — of duty, knowledge, effort, grace and truth.

## When it is read

Japji is the heart of the morning nitnem at *amrit vela*, the still hours before dawn that [Sikh practice](/faith/foundations-philosophy) prizes for remembrance, and it is among the banis recited in the preparation of amrit [at initiation](/faith/ceremonies-and-rites). Many Sikhs know it entire by heart.
