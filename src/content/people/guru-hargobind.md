## Two swords at the succession

Eleven years old at his father's [death under torture](/events/martyrdom-of-guru-arjan), Hargobind came to the Guruship in 1606 wearing two swords — *miri* for temporal authority, *piri* for spiritual: the doctrine of [miri-piri](/glossary/miri-piri) that reset the community's course. He kept an armed retinue, hunted, and [raised the Akal Takht](/events/founding-of-akal-takht) facing the Harmandir Sahib — sanctity and sovereignty in one compound.

## Prison and the fifty-two kings

The new posture brought imperial suspicion, and the Guru spent a period imprisoned in Gwalior Fort under Jahangir. The tradition of his release — refusing freedom unless fifty-two imprisoned rajas walked out holding the tassels of his cloak — is kept each year at Bandi Chhor Divas, the Sikh Diwali, when [the Golden Temple](/places/amritsar) is lit in remembrance of the liberator of captives.

## First battles, and a move to the hills

Under Shah Jahan, friction became fighting: Mughal detachments engaged the Guru's men near Amritsar in 1634 and at Kartarpur in 1635, and the Sikhs — for the first time under arms — held their own. The Guru withdrew to Kiratpur in the Shivalik foothills, where he died in 1644, having chosen his young grandson [Har Rai](/people/guru-har-rai) to succeed him. The [martial tradition](/culture/martial-tradition) he founded never again left the faith.
